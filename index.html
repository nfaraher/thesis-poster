Background

As partisan animosity and political violence rise in the United States, questions of causation arise. Psychological research lends that dehumanization may be the causal gateway.

▸ Three Related, Non-Identical Constructs
Negative Partisanship → Affective Polarization → Partisan Animosity…

Negative Partisanship (Abramowitz & Webster, 2016) — structural/motivational upstream condition. Partisan identity organized around opposition. Webster (2020) American Rage: this anger erodes democratic norms; elites sustain it for electoral loyalty.

Affective Polarization (Iyengar et al., 2012) — inparty warmth minus outparty coldness spread. What V-DEM v2cacamps captures in Study 3.

Study 1 — Takeaway

Across dozens of independent studies, the indirect path from partisan hostility through dehumanization to violence is consistently stronger than the direct hostility-to-violence path. The literature as a whole points toward dehumanization as the critical link — hatred alone does not reliably produce violence endorsement.

Study 2 — Dehumanization as Mediator

IV = partisan animosity (outparty thermometer coldness, 0–100). U.S. only, 2018–2020.

▸ Central Implication for Intervention
Reducing hatred alone may not be enough — dehumanization must be directly addressed.

The suppressor structure means programs that reduce partisan animosity broadly — cross-partisan contact, media literacy, depolarization campaigns — may not interrupt the violence pathway if they leave dehumanizing cognitive representations intact. The psychological lever is the specific step of perceiving outpartisans as less than fully human. Reducing contempt without restoring recognition of full humanity may leave the violence-enabling mechanism untouched. The intervention target is dehumanization specifically, not hostility generally.

▸ Scope & Limitations
U.S.-specific, correlational, 2018–2020 — generalization requires caution.

Study 2 is U.S.-only data collected 2018–2020. All individual-level findings are correlational; causal claims depend on theoretical priors, not experimental manipulation. The SDO asymmetry is an observed correlation, not a demonstrated mechanism. Study 1 mixes effect size types across studies (r, β, d); pooled estimates are heuristic. Study 3 uses observational panel data; reverse causation (violence driving polarization) cannot be ruled out. SEs are approximate — country-clustered standard errors would be larger.

Study 2 — Takeaway

Hating the other side does not directly cause people to endorse violence — but it does cause them to see the other side as less than human. That dehumanization is what produces the violence endorsement. Remove the dehumanization step and the link from hatred to violence essentially disappears.

Study 3 — Takeaway

Across 118 democracies over 75 years, countries that become more politically polarized reliably see more political violence — regardless of region, era, or regime type. The United States has sat at the maximum recorded polarization for approximately 8 years; these global results externally validate the study and warn us that understanding how animosity becomes violence couldn't be more relevant.

What This Research Tells Us
IDecades of research confirm that dehumanization sits between partisan hatred and violence — the indirect pathway consistently outweighs the direct one across 37 independent effect sizes.
IIIn a U.S. sample of 2,003 respondents, dehumanization fully explains the hatred–violence link. The effect holds across 12 different measures of violence endorsement and is roughly twice as strong among Republicans.
IIIGlobally, polarization predicts political violence across 118 democracies over 75 years — and the U.S. has never been more polarized.
